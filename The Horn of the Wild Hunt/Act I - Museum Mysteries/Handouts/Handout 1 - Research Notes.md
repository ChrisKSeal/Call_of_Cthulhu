

## Celtic Bloodline Research - October 1925

**Primary Focus**: Tracing unbroken genealogical lines from pre-Roman British Celtic families to contemporary descendants

The work Edwin and I have undertaken represents perhaps the most comprehensive study of Celtic hereditary patterns in the British Isles. What began as simple genealogical research has revealed disturbing patterns that stretch back over a millennium. These are not mere family trees, but rather bloodlines that carry something far more significant than noble heritage.

**Key Families Identified**:

The Whitmore line (Yorkshire) presents the most complete documentation, with parish records, land grants, and family documents creating an unbroken chain extending back approximately one thousand years. The family has maintained continuous residence near the Thornborough Henges, with the eldest child of each generation inheriting not only property but what local records euphemistically term "guardianship duties."

The Carstairs family (Scottish Borders) shows remarkably similar patterns. Edwin's own research into his ancestry revealed connections to pre-Roman Celtic settlements along Hadrian's Wall. His family records speak of "the old obligations" and reference seasonal rituals that must be maintained "lest the barriers fail."

The Blackwood lineage (Northumberland) combines ancient druidic connections with a strong military tradition. Parish records from the 14th century onward consistently show Blackwood men serving as "protectors of the sacred groves" alongside their martial duties. The current heir, Captain Thomas Blackwood, recently returned from the Great War, though local reports suggest he has become something of a hermit.

**Genealogical Anomalies**:

What troubles me most deeply is not the antiquity of these bloodlines, but their consistency. Normal family trees show the expected variations - marriages outside the region, children who died young, branches that ended or moved away. These families show an almost supernatural tendency to maintain their bloodlines within specific geographical boundaries.

More disturbing still are the recurring patterns of disappearance. Every family shows cyclical losses, typically occurring around Samhain, with individuals simply vanishing from historical records. Parish registers consistently note these disappearances with phrases such as "taken by the Wild Hunt," "claimed by Hearne's Hounds," or "answered the ancient call."

The pattern repeats with mathematical precision across centuries. The Whitmore family alone shows documented disappearances in 1823, 1810, 1797, 1784 - exactly thirteen years apart, always in late October, always involving the family member who had reached their thirtieth year.

**The Bloodline Curse**:

Local folklore, which I initially dismissed as peasant superstition, now appears to contain disturbing kernels of truth. The families consistently referred to as "Hearne's Children" or "marked by the Hunt" are precisely those whose genealogies show the unbroken Celtic connections.

The curse, if such it may be called, appears to activate around the age of thirty. Family members begin experiencing vivid dreams of hunting horns and baying hounds. They report feeling compelled to visit ancient sites during full moons. Physical changes begin - enhanced night vision, unusual sensitivity to iron, and in some cases, small growths resembling antler buds appearing at the temples.

The final stage involves what local records term "the calling." The affected individual becomes increasingly drawn to sacred groves and stone circles. They begin speaking fragments of what appears to be ancient Gaelic or even older Celtic dialects. Eventually, they simply disappear, usually found days later in ritual positions near ancient sites, if found at all.

**Personal Observations**:

I must record here, for the sake of scientific honesty, that I have begun experiencing some of these phenomena myself. The dreams began in July - always the same vision of a vast forest where antlered figures move between ancient oaks. I wake with the sound of hunting horns echoing in my ears and the taste of autumn leaves on my tongue.

More troubling, I find myself drawn to visit the Celtic collection after hours. Last week, I discovered that I had spent three hours in the basement storage areas with no memory of going there. The security logs show I accessed the restricted sections using my authorization, but I have no recollection of the visit.

The compulsion grows stronger as October advances. I understand now why mother moved our family to London when I was a child, why she forbade any mention of our Yorkshire heritage. She was trying to break the cycle, to sever our connection to the old ways.

But the blood remembers what the mind forgets. The curse is not bound by geography or denial. It will have what it is owed.

## Hearne/Herne Research Notes - October 1925

The earliest references to what we now term "Herne the Hunter" appear in 14th-century manuscripts from Windsor Forest, though the name itself shows clear signs of linguistic corruption from much older origins. The Windsor Chronicle of 1387 describes encounters with "Herne ye Huntsman," a spectral figure who leads ghostly hounds through the royal hunting grounds during winter storms.

However, comparative analysis with Continental sources reveals striking similarities to the Romano-Celtic deity Cernunnos. The Gundestrup Cauldron's depictions of the antlered god match precisely with English folklore descriptions of Herne's physical appearance - the massive antlered figure seated in a position of divine authority, surrounded by wild beasts.

The transformation follows predictable phonetic patterns observed in other Celtic-to-English linguistic shifts:

Original Celtic: Cernunnos (pronounced "Ker-NOO-nos") - meaning "The Horned One"  
Romano-British Period: Cernunnos/Kernunnos - Romanized spellings  
Early Medieval: Cern/Kern - shortened forms in local dialects  
Middle English: Herne - phonetic adaptation influenced by Germanic linguistic patterns  
Modern Regional: Hearne - particularly prevalent in Yorkshire and northern dialects

What troubles me deeply is the consistency of this evolution across disparate regions. One would expect random variation in folk etymology, yet the progression appears almost deliberately guided. Local dialects from Yorkshire to Cornwall consistently use "Hearne" rather than the literary "Herne," suggesting a common source for this particular pronunciation.

I have begun to suspect that "Hearne" is not merely a dialectical corruption, but rather the preservation of the true name through oral tradition. Academic sources favor "Herne" or "Cernnous" because it appears in written medieval texts, but the common folk who maintained the actual traditions consistently use "Hearne."

Despite the name variations, descriptions remain remarkably uniform across centuries and regions:

- Massive humanoid figure bearing the antlers of a great stag
- Commands a pack of supernatural hounds, often described as "shadow-black" or "wreathed in mist"
- Associated with winter storms and the period around Samhain when the veil between worlds grows thin
- Leads what medieval sources term the "Wild Hunt" - a spectral cavalcade that claims the souls of the living
- Connected to ancient groves, stone circles, and burial sites of pre-Roman origin

The consistency suggests we are dealing not with evolving folklore, but with persistent encounters with the same entity across generations.

My research has uncovered contemporary accounts that mirror the medieval descriptions with unsettling accuracy. Parish records from Yorkshire, particularly around the Thornborough area, consistently report sightings of the "Wild Hunt" during October and November. These are not quaint folklore tales, but sworn testimonies from farmers, clergy, and local officials.

The evidence increasingly suggests that Herne/Hearne/Cernunnos represents not a mythological construct, but a persistent supernatural entity that has maintained influence over British soil for millennia. The linguistic evolution appears designed to obscure this truth while preserving the essential knowledge among specific bloodlines.

If my suspicions are correct, then the current research Edwin and I are conducting may have attracted unwanted attention. The dreams grow stronger, and I fear we have stumbled upon secrets that were meant to remain buried in the mists of Celtic antiquity.

The old ways demand their due, and I suspect payment is coming due very soon.

## The Thornborough Connection - October 1925


The deeper I delve into my family's history, the more I understand that what I once dismissed as quaint Yorkshire folklore represents something far more significant and terrifying. The Whitmore lineage is not merely connected to the Thornborough Henges - we are bound to them by chains forged in blood and ancient oath, stretching back through the mists of Celtic antiquity.

The Whitmore family has maintained continuous residence near the henges for over nine centuries, an unbroken chain of possession that defies the normal patterns of medieval land tenure. While other families rose and fell with political fortunes, lost holdings to war or marriage, or simply moved to more prosperous regions, the Whitmores remained. The land grants I've examined show a curious consistency - each generation's heir receives not only property rights but specific obligations described in archaic legal language as maintenance of the old covenants and guardianship of the sacred boundaries.

What troubles me most deeply is the precision of these obligations. Medieval documents typically contain vague religious language about Christian duties or feudal service. The Whitmore grants, however, specify exact dates for ritual observances, particular groves that must be maintained, and even the requirement that family members possess knowledge of the ancient scripts.

I now understand that what I interpreted as family eccentricity was actually the manifestation of an hereditary curse - or perhaps more accurately, a mystical obligation that has become corrupted over time. The dreams that began plaguing me in July are not random nightmares but inherited memories, passed down through bloodline from generation to generation.

The progression follows a predictable pattern. Around the age of thirty, family members begin experiencing vivid dreams of ancient forests where antlered figures move between standing stones. They are then recorded as having disappeared after Samhaim.

My dreams are accompanied by an inexplicable knowledge of Ogham script - I find myself reading inscriptions I've never studied, understanding words in a language I've never learned.



Something has gone terribly wrong with the family's ancient role. We were meant to be guardians, maintaining the barriers between worlds, ensuring that certain entities remained bound in their otherworldly realms. But someone - some force or faction - has corrupted this sacred duty, turning protection into summoning, binding into release.

The cult that has emerged around the old traditions seeks not to maintain the ancient compact but to shatter it entirely. They have identified the key bloodlines, learned the ritual requirements, and begun the process of turning our hereditary obligation into the very mechanism of our destruction. They need our blood, our knowledge, our willing or unwilling participation to complete their work.

Time grows short. The astronomical conditions will be perfect for their purposes on Samhain night, when the veil between worlds reaches its thinnest point. If they succeed in their great working, the Wild Hunt will ride not as a distant supernatural phenomenon but as a present, terrible reality that will sweep across Britain like a plague of shadows and death.

## Ritual Components - URGENT RESEARCH - October 1925

**The Sacred Cauldron and Its Terrible Purpose**

My investigation into the ritual implements required for the great summoning has revealed details that fill me with both fascination and horror. The cult has not simply improvised their ceremonies but has instead corrupted genuine Celtic religious practices, turning sacred rites of protection into mechanisms of supernatural destruction.

**The Cauldron of Rebirth - Pair Dadeni**:

The bronze vessel currently housed in our museum's restricted storage represents one of the legendary Thirteen Treasures of Britain. Medieval Welsh texts describe the Pair Dadeni as a cauldron capable of restoring the dead to life, though the resurrected would return without the power of speech. What the ancient texts fail to mention - or perhaps deliberately obscure - is the terrible price required for such resurrection.

The cauldron's interior bears Ogham inscriptions that I have only recently learned to decipher. These markings detail the specific requirements for its operation: the vessel must be filled with the blood of individuals possessing unbroken druidic lineage, collected according to precise ritual specifications, and mixed with sacred herbs gathered during specific lunar phases.

Edwin's recent examination of the artifact revealed disturbing evidence of recent use. Microscopic analysis showed traces of human blood mixed with salt, dried mistletoe, oak bark, and rowan berries - exactly the combination specified in the Ogham instructions. Someone with museum access has been conducting preliminary rituals using this priceless archaeological treasure as an active religious implement.

**The Sacrificial Requirements and Methodology**:

The Ogham texts describe in horrifying detail the preparation of sacrificial victims. The chosen individual must be suspended upside-down above the cauldron, allowing for complete blood drainage when the throat is cut with a bronze blade blessed according to ancient rites. This positioning ensures that every drop of blood flows into the vessel while the victim remains conscious for as long as possible - apparently the psychological terror and spiritual anguish of the dying person adds potency to the ritual.

Following the blood collection, the corpse must be packed with a specific mixture of preservative materials. Rock salt, harvested from coastal regions during the new moon, forms the primary preservation medium. This is combined with mistletoe cut from oak trees using golden sickles, oak bark stripped during thunderstorms, and rowan berries gathered at dawn on the autumn equinox.

The preserved body, when properly prepared, maintains a mystical connection to the blood contained in the cauldron. This connection allows for the corpse's reanimation if the cauldron's contents are disturbed, creating what the texts term a "guardian of the sacred vessel." More disturbingly, the preservation process appears to maintain not only the body's physical integrity but also fragments of the victim's consciousness, trapped in a state between life and death.

**Bloodline Requirements and Mystical Genetics**:

The ritual's effectiveness depends entirely on the genetic heritage of the sacrificial victims. Only individuals possessing unbroken lineage to pre-Roman Celtic religious families can provide the necessary mystical potency. The cult has clearly conducted extensive genealogical research, identifying specific family lines that carry the required spiritual marking.

The Whitmore, Carstairs, and Blackwood families represent prime examples of these bloodlines. Our ancestors served as druids, maintaining the religious traditions that bound supernatural entities and protected Britain from otherworldly intrusions. This hereditary responsibility has been passed down through generations, carried in our very blood whether we acknowledge it or not.

What makes our situation particularly dire is that the cult requires willing participation from the bloodline families. Forced sacrifice, while possible, lacks the spiritual authority necessary for the great summoning. They need us to choose to participate, to willingly offer our blood and lives for their purpose. This explains their careful psychological manipulation, their exploitation of family curses and hereditary obligations.

**Astronomical Timing and Samhain's Significance**:

The final ritual must occur during Samhain, when the barrier between the mortal world and the Otherworld reaches its annual minimum. Celtic tradition recognizes this night as the time when spirits walk freely among the living, when divination achieves its greatest accuracy, and when the rules governing supernatural entities temporarily weaken.

The specific timing requires even greater precision than simply occurring on October 31st. The ritual must begin when the moon reaches its zenith above the ancient burial chamber at Maeshowe, creating a perfect alignment between celestial and terrestrial sacred sites. This alignment occurs only once each year, and the approaching Samhain represents the first time in decades when all other conditions will also be favorable.

**The Herbs and Their Properties**:

Each component of the preservation mixture serves both practical and mystical purposes:

Mistletoe, particularly when harvested from oak trees, has been sacred to Celtic peoples since time immemorial. The druids considered it a bridge between earth and sky, capable of opening doorways between worlds. Its inclusion in the preservation mixture creates mystical channels through which supernatural entities can manifest.

Oak bark, especially when gathered during thunderstorms, carries the electrical essence of the storm god's power. Celtic tradition associates oak trees with divine strength and endurance, making the bark an essential component for rituals requiring long-term mystical effects.

Rowan berries provide protection against malevolent spirits while simultaneously attracting benevolent otherworldly entities. Their inclusion in the mixture ensures that the ritual attracts the specific supernatural forces the cult wishes to summon while repelling random spiritual interference.

Rock salt serves as both preservative and purifier, maintaining the physical integrity of the sacrifice while cleansing the ritual space of competing mystical influences. The requirement that it be harvested during the new moon ensures maximum spiritual potency.

**Personal Observations and Growing Terror**:

As I write these notes, I can feel the pull of the ancient obligations growing stronger. The knowledge of these rituals feels familiar rather than learned, as if I am remembering rather than discovering. This troubles me deeply - it suggests that the bloodline carries not only genetic markers but actual inherited memories of these practices.

Edwin has not returned from his late-night examination of the cauldron. His absence, combined with my growing understanding of the ritual requirements, fills me with dread. I fear he may have become the cult's first sacrifice, his blood now filling the ancient vessel as preparation for the greater working to come.

Time grows desperately short. If my calculations are correct, the cult will attempt their great summoning in just days. The astronomical conditions will be perfect, they have access to the necessary ritual implements, and they have identified the bloodlines required for success. All that remains is gathering the final sacrifices and transporting the operation to the sacred site where the barriers between worlds are thinnest.

I must reach the puzzle box's destination before they complete their preparations. Whatever ancient compact my family made centuries ago, whatever obligation we carry in our blood, I believe it was meant to prevent exactly this kind of supernatural catastrophe. The knowledge and authority I've inherited may be the only force capable of stopping what is coming.

May God preserve us all from the consequences of ancient oaths and the hunger of things that should remain forever bound in darkness.
