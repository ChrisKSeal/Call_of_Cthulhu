**15th July, 1925**

<THIS SHOULD BE A THING FOUND AFTER A BREAKIN INTO THE MUSEUM>
What a delightful mystery Edwin has brought me! A bronze puzzle box from grandfather's estate - seven rings covered in the most exquisite Celtic knotwork, each inscribed with what appear to be Ogham letters. I've spent the better part of the evening turning the rings this way and that, trying to divine the correct combination. It's rather like having a conversation with the ancient Celts themselves, isn't it?

There's something almost musical about the way the bronze rings click against each other. "Seven letters for seven rings," I told Edwin over tea. "There must be a word - a name, perhaps - that serves as the key." He laughed and said I sound more like a treasure hunter than a proper academic. Perhaps he's right, but where's the harm in a little romance with the past?

Mother always said grandfather was "touched" when it came to his Celtic interests. Looking at this beautiful artifact, I begin to think his passion was more scholarly than eccentric.

**22nd July, 1925**

The family genealogy project has taken an unexpected turn. I began simply trying to trace our Yorkshire roots, but the records go back much further than I anticipated. The Whitmore name appears consistently in Thornborough parish records dating to the Norman Conquest - not just living in the area, but holding the exact same lands for nearly nine centuries.

That's remarkable persistence for any family. Most medieval holdings changed hands regularly through war, marriage, or economic misfortune. Yet the Whitmores seem to have maintained their connection to those specific lands with almost supernatural consistency.

The legal documents contain curious references to "guardianship duties" and "maintenance of ancient covenants" that seem oddly specific for typical land tenure agreements. What manner of guardianship could require such precise legal language century after century?

**5th August, 1925**

I've been having the most vivid dreams lately - vast forests of oak and ash, stone circles gleaming in moonlight, and always the sense of something magnificent watching from the shadows. They're not unpleasant, exactly, but they leave me with such a peculiar longing when I wake.

The genealogical work has revealed a troubling pattern. Every few centuries - roughly every 300 to 400 years - a member of the Whitmore family simply vanishes from the historical record around Samhain. Not deaths with proper burials, mind you, but complete disappearances. The parish records use the most extraordinary language: "taken by the Wild Hunt," "answered the ancient call," "claimed by Hearne's blessing."

It's probably just medieval superstition, but the precision of the timing is unsettling. As a researcher, I'm trained to look for patterns, and this one is too consistent to dismiss as coincidence.

**18th August, 1925**

I've solved the puzzle box, though I confess the solution came to me in a dream rather than through scholarly deduction. The contents were... illuminating. And deeply troubling.

I've returned everything to its proper place and sealed the mechanism once more. Some knowledge, I'm beginning to understand, carries a weight that transforms the bearer simply by existing in their consciousness.

The dreams have intensified since opening the box. I wake with the taste of autumn leaves and the sound of distant hunting horns echoing in my memory. Most peculiar of all, I find myself looking forward to sleep, eager to return to those ancient woodlands where something waits with infinite patience.

This troubles me greatly. I am a woman of science, trained to approach mysteries with rational skepticism. Yet I cannot shake the feeling that I've awakened something that was better left dormant.

**31st August, 1925**

The contents of the puzzle box revealed the truth about my family's role, and I confess I am both terrified and... disturbed by my own reaction to this knowledge.

We were never simple landowners. The Whitmore bloodline carries something far more significant - and dangerous. Every 300 to 400 years, when certain astronomical conditions align and the barriers between worlds grow thin, one of our line is... required. Not to protect or maintain, as I initially believed, but to serve as a component in a ritual of unimaginable consequence.

My rational mind recoils from this knowledge. Everything I've learned about proper research methodology screams that this is fantasy, delusion projected onto coincidental patterns.

Yet there's something else stirring within me - a recognition that feels older than conscious thought. It whispers that this revelation is not discovery but remembrance, as if some part of me has always known this truth.

This internal conflict terrifies me more than any external threat could.

**15th September, 1925**

I am losing the battle with whatever force stirs in my blood.

Each morning I wake more convinced of my scholarly skepticism, determined to approach this mystery with proper scientific method. I make careful notes about the psychological effects of suggestion and the human tendency to find patterns in random events.

But as the day progresses, that rational voice grows quieter, overwhelmed by a certainty that seems to pulse through my veins. I find myself reading about ancient Celtic sacrifice rituals not with academic detachment but with what I can only describe as anticipation.

This is not me. This is not the woman who spent years learning to separate emotion from analysis, who built her career on methodical observation and logical deduction. Yet I cannot deny that some part of me - a part that grows stronger each day - feels honored by the role my bloodline is meant to play.

The conflict is tearing me apart. In my clearer moments, I recognize that I may be suffering from some form of psychological breakdown. The alternative - that I am genuinely experiencing some hereditary compulsion toward ritual sacrifice - is too horrifying to contemplate.

**28th September, 1925**

The calling has become undeniable, and my resistance grows weaker each day.

I wake each morning with the taste of sacred groves on my tongue and the sound of hunting horns echoing in my ears. Every fiber of my being yearns to travel north, to fulfill some purpose I understand instinctively but cannot rationally accept.

What terrifies me most is not the compulsion itself, but how right it feels. In my moments of greatest clarity, I can see that I'm losing myself to something ancient and inexorable. Yet even as I recognize this transformation, part of me welcomes it with a joy so pure it takes my breath away.

I am a scholar. I have dedicated my life to understanding the past through evidence and reason. I should be fighting this influence with every tool at my disposal.

Instead, I find myself researching the routes to northern Scotland and beyond.

The rational part of my mind screams that I'm making a terrible mistake, that I'm walking voluntarily toward my own destruction. But that voice is being overwhelmed by something that feels more fundamental than thought - a calling that resonates in my bones and blood.

I fear I am going mad. I fear even more that I am not.

**5th October, 1925**

Edwin has disappeared.

He came to see me yesterday, clearly concerned about my state of mind. I must have said something that alarmed him - though I can barely remember our conversation. The days blur together now, rational thought becoming increasingly difficult to maintain.

When I woke this morning, he was gone. His notebook lies open on my desk, but Edwin himself has vanished without explanation.

I should be worried. I should be searching for him, contacting the authorities, doing something to help my dear friend and colleague.

Instead, I feel only a distant sadness and a growing certainty that his disappearance is connected to forces beyond my understanding or control. The rational part of my mind - that increasingly small voice of scientific skepticism - whispers that I should flee, that I should fight whatever influence has taken hold of me.

But I know I won't. I know I can't.

Something in my blood sings with ancient purpose, and despite my terror, despite my desperate attempts to maintain rational thought, I find myself making preparations for a journey I do not consciously wish to take.

**10th October, 1925**

I can no longer distinguish between my own thoughts and the compulsions that drive me.

The woman who began this journal - the scholar, the researcher, the rational academic - watches in horror as her body makes preparations for travel north. I have researched passage routes. I have gathered supplies. I have done things I cannot remember deciding to do.

Yet even as I recognize my loss of control, part of me thrums with anticipation. The conflict within me has become a war, and I fear the rational mind is losing.

The autumn grows deeper, and I sense that time grows short. Something approaches - some convergence of forces that my blood recognizes even as my mind rejects it.

I want to fight this. I want to resist. I want to be the woman of science I trained myself to become.

But the calling grows stronger with each passing hour, and my will to resist grows correspondingly weaker.

I fear I know where this path leads, and I fear even more that I lack the strength to step away from it.

_[The final entry is written in a noticeably shakier hand]:_

I can hear them now - horns in the distance, sounds that seem to come from somewhere beyond the normal world. My rational mind makes one last, desperate attempt to assert control, to convince me that I'm suffering from some kind of breakdown.

But I know better. Deep in my bones, in some part of my consciousness that predates rational thought, I know exactly what approaches.

I am terrified. I am exhilarated. I am no longer entirely myself.

The compulsion to travel north grows stronger each day. I have begun making concrete preparations for a journey I do not wish to take but cannot avoid.

God help me, I think my fate is sealed.
